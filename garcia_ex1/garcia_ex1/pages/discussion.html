GREECE

History of Greece

The history of Greece spans over four thousand years, beginning with the early civilizations of the Minoans and the Mycenaeans, continuing on to the Classical period with the emergence of Athens as the dominant city-state in the fifth century BCE, and finally arriving at the Hellenistic period with the conquests of Alexander the Great and the spread of Greek culture throughout the Mediterranean world.

During the Bronze Age, the Minoan civilization flourished on the island of Crete, while the Mycenaean civilization dominated the Greek mainland. Both of these civilizations were located in Greece. The latter failed around the year 1200 BCE, marking the beginning of a period of relative instability known as the Greek Dark Ages.

City-states began to appear around the eighth century BCE, which coincided with a period of revitalized economic expansion and cultural advancement. A series of wars, including the Persian Wars and the Peloponnesian War, broke out in the region as a result of the rise of Athens, Sparta, Corinth, and Thebes as the preeminent powers in that area.

Athens went through a renaissance in the fields of art, literature, philosophy, and democracy during the time period known as the Classical period. This was made possible by the leadership of statesmen such as Pericles. The city's power, on the other hand, began to wane after it was defeated in the Peloponnesian War and then subsequently subjugated by the Macedonian king Philip II.

Alexander the Great, the son of Philip II, was responsible for the greatest expansion of the Greek empire and was responsible for the conquest of a significant portion of the known world before his death in 323 BCE. After his death, the period that followed saw the establishment of Hellenistic kingdoms in Egypt, Syria, and Asia Minor.

The Romans were eventually successful in their conquest of Greece, which took place in the second century BCE, and Greece was subsequently incorporated into the Roman Empire. In spite of its incorporation into the Roman world, Greek culture continued to flourish during the Roman period, and many of the world's most influential works of literature, philosophy, and science were written in Greek during this time period.

The culture and language of Greece had a significant impact on the development of the Byzantine Empire, which arose in the wake of the collapse of the Western Roman Empire. Constantinople served as the seat of the Eastern Orthodox Church, which played an important part in the administration of the empire (modern-day Istanbul).

Greece finally won its independence from Ottoman rule in the 19th century, after being subjected to it for many centuries, and established itself as a constitutional monarchy. During World War II, the country was occupied by Nazi Germany; however, it eventually regained its independence and transformed into a republic in the year 1974. Greece is a country in Europe that is a member of the European Union and is famous for the natural beauty of its islands as well as its rich cultural history.
